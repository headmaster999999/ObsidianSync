# notes

proceeding from the notion of photography as a vector of depersonalization, violence, Kyoshi Sugita's A Song I Remember inverts the typical direction of victimization away from other and towards the self. The protagonist Takeshi is an amateur photographer who obsessively stalks tokyo subway platforms with his polaroid camera. the impression we get is that, Takeshis (a man of few words, discerning in nature), photography practice is a personal act of adopting the external worlds phenomena into his inner world---in his process of interperetation, possessing objects that lay in the strange world beyond himself via his camera allows for its sublimation in into domesticated furniture in his inner world. He can capture his polaroid pictures (the most instant of film photography) and look on them in the controlled conditions of the films frame. A peaceful practice, one that makes a frenzied city appear almost bucolic, the eerie and hostile into pets---one night venture introduces a new dimension of photography into his understanding (this is so dogshit lol). Catching interest in a woman waiting on the arrival on her train, flipping through a book---he takes her photo. satisfied in the portrait of her he now possesses, he leaves her. On his way up the platform stairs towards the exit, he notice a suspicious looking man pass him on his own route now in reverse. Unassuming, he the ascends the stairs upwards. It is only on the upper level of the subway station that alarms blare, subway breaks shriek, station attendents with sterile voices announce an incident on the subway line: theres been an accident. Someones fallen onto the tracks.  Takeshi, investigation the sudden commotion, descends the platform stairs. the strange man from before passes him the opposite way at vigorous pace---almost fleeing. we ascertain that the woman in the portrait Takeshis holds has been pushed onto the tracks to be crushed under oncoming traincars.  

with the foresight of the blind seer who sees so much, Takeshi produces a ghost. in snapping photo Takeshi enters into the photographer-photograph dialectic. Takeshi, holding the likeness of a woman now mangled---her essence nowhere except approximated in the parody contra-universe of photographs---in his hands, is now caretaker, guardian of a helpless spirit.
now entered into the pact, obligated to the honor of his photo-brood, a helpless thing---hes bound to documenting the drama to full extent. Takeshi, with a sleepwalkers fascination---waits the moment obligatory of discretion, and follows.

Trailing the man station after station. He finds him in a desolate street in the dark. Unable to resist, he raises his camera and takes the mans photo. Hearing the mechanical shutter capturing his likeness, the man turns to meets takeshis gaze. They part.

now with both portraits, victim and perpetrator, stuffed together into a bag together, all thats left is the interperetation, sublimation---now no longer synthesis offspring of positive dialectic, but a negative one. Not liberation, burden.

Takeshi stands furtively outside a police station, watching the policemen. The stakes are suicide. To invite the poetryless penal code bureaucrats into the fold is to turn his inner-life of holy images into life laid bare of legal evidence is nothing less than self-annihilation. He turns and leaves---the twisted logic of the photographer: all the justice that needs deliverance is in capturing the perpetrator in his own photo prison---a sentencing of eternity to languish in his film-cell.

he goes home with the burden of justice. Harbors it in himself like a martyr. 

it is only later that, by pure chance, he meets the daughter of the murdered woman.
